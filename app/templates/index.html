Early Detection is Everything

A Life Lost Every 17 Seconds

Every 17 seconds, lung cancer claims a life, totaling more than 1.8 million deaths each year. It remains one of the deadliest forms of cancer, not because it's the most common, but because it's often detected too late. The disease typically progresses without noticeable symptoms, making early treatment difficult.

4 Out of 5 Don't Survive

Lung cancer remains one of the most lethal cancers, with only one in five people surviving beyond five years. For every five diagnoses, four lives are lost. This is largely due to late detection, when treatment is less effective. Unlike other cancers with routine screening, lung cancer often spreads silently.

Less Common, More Deadly

Lung cancer is the third most diagnosed cancer in the U.S., yet far more deadly than breast or prostate cancer. While those cancers have survival-friendly ratios over 6 to 1, lung cancer's is just 1.77 to 1, nearly one death for every two diagnoses. This highlights not only the disease's aggressiveness but also the challenges of early detection.
